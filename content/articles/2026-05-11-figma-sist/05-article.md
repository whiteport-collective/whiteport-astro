# Why Are Designers So Uninterested in AI?

**The numbers tell a story nobody wants to hear.**

---

**Image:** Infographic. A grid of dots — 300 total — each colored by group. Grey: did not respond (100). Light red: said no thank you (100). Yellow: signed up (25). Green: showed up (3). Clean, minimal, brutal.

---

I reached out to 300 designers.

I offered them a free, practical, hands-on introduction to agentic design. No pitch. No sales. Just a morning of learning how to work with AI agents the way designers actually think — starting from strategy, building through wireframes and specifications, arriving at working code.

The response stunned me.

100 people never replied.

100 said no thank you.

25 signed up.

3 showed up.

---

*One person wrote back: "I have too much work jobsearching so I don't have time to learn AI right now."*

---

Let that land for a moment.

Jobsearching. Too busy looking for work to learn the skill that is actively reshaping what work means in this field.

## This is not an isolated story

When I talk to design leaders — and I talk to a lot of them — I hear the same thing, worded slightly differently, over and over.

The design team is resistant.

A design director at a major company told me that every attempt to introduce AI-assisted workflows is met with a kind of passive non-cooperation. Designers don't refuse outright. They just don't adopt. They try it once, find it imperfect, and return to the way they've always worked.

A head of design at a fintech told me his team spent three months trialing an AI workflow tool, produced zero real deliverables, and concluded that "the tool isn't ready yet."

The tool isn't ready. That phrase keeps coming back.

## Is it fear, or is it something deeper?

I've thought about this a lot.

The easy answer is fear. Designers are afraid of being replaced, so they avoid the thing they fear might replace them. Avoidance as a coping strategy.

But I don't think it's that simple.

I think the resistance runs deeper. I think it's about identity.

Design is a craft. Designers spent years developing fluency in a visual language, in the tools of that language, in the rituals of that craft. Sketch notes. Whiteboard sessions. Critiques. The language of a good wireframe review. These are not just workflows. They are who designers are.

AI doesn't just threaten the job. It threatens the identity.

To adopt AI is to admit that some of the things that made you special can now be automated. And that is a genuinely hard thing to face.

## I refuse to accept that designers are going to stop mattering

Here is what I know to be true.

No AI agent woke up this morning with a burning insight about what users actually need. No system trained on pattern completion has ever felt the moment in a user interview when someone stops talking and looks away because the thing they're trying to say is too embarrassing to say out loud. No model knows what it means when a user says "it's fine" and means "I gave up."

Designers know these things. They live in these things.

The question is not whether designers are needed. Of course they are.

The question is whether designers will do the hard work of evolving — from pixel craftspeople into the strategic, agent-commanding, clarity-generating professionals the agentic era requires.

The resistance is understandable. The stakes are also real.

300 invitations. 3 people in the room.

I am not giving up on designers. I am asking designers to not give up on themselves.

The world is not waiting.
