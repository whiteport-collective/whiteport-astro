## LinkedIn draft

Pre-AI, micro-management was an effective (if expensive) safeguard.

You spend hundreds of thousands and three months on a project — of *course* you book status meetings, sign-offs, design reviews, executive walkthroughs. The cost of constant oversight is small relative to the cost of the project going sideways.

Post-AI, micro-management kills adoption dead.

You run an agent for 30 seconds.
Then book a 60-minute meeting to approve what it produced.

It doesn't fly.

The math is broken. The agent's value depends on it being allowed to *run*. Once you slot every output into your old approval pipeline, you've replaced one bottleneck (manual work) with another (permission). The team realizes faster than you do.

This is what governance actually solves.

A real framework says:
✅ Each person can decide *these* things alone
✅ Teams handle *those* things internally
✅ Only *these specific* situations escalate
✅ Here's what happens if something breaks

Once that's written down, signed, and shared — your people stop waiting. They move.

That's the paradox of governance: more structure = more freedom. More guardrails = more speed. More boundaries = more autonomy.

If your team is still booking 1:1s to approve agent outputs — your governance is missing.
