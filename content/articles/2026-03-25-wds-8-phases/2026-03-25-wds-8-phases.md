# WDS — The End-to-End AI Agent Framework for Design Teams

## Syfte

Hjälpa design managers som utvärderar AI-ramverk att förstå att WDS inte är ännu ett prompt-verktyg — det är en komplett pipeline från strategi till designsystem. Positionera WDS som det enda ramverket som täcker hela kedjan, och visa varför strategi-först-approachen (Trigger Mapping) är den hemliga ingrediensen.

## Målgrupp

Design managers och design leads som:
- Utvärderar AI-ramverk för sina team
- Är trötta på fragmenterade AI-verktyg som löser ett steg i taget
- Vill ha en metodik, inte bara fler verktyg
- Behöver övertyga sin organisation om att AI-design handlar om mer än snabba wireframes

## Pillar

Knowledge — "One insight per post. Not a system, not a framework. ONE thing."
Insikten här: **ett AI-ramverk måste täcka hela pipelinen, inte bara pixlarna.**

## Nyckelpoänger

1. **Fragmenterade AI-verktyg löser steg, inte problem.** Du limmar fortfarande ihop 15 verktyg själv.
2. **Strategi först.** De flesta AI-ramverk börjar med "generera wireframe." WDS börjar med "varför finns den här produkten?"
3. **Trigger Map finns ingenstans annars.** Kartlägger användarpsykologi innan design börjar. Varje beslut spåras tillbaka till ett riktigt användarbehov.
4. **1 process, 8 faser, inte 8 verktyg.** En sammanhängande pipeline där varje fas matar nästa.
5. **Human-guided AI.** Agenter föreslår, människor beslutar. Full kontroll, 10x hastighet.
6. **Open source, vendor-oberoende.** MIT-licensierat. Kör med vilken AI-modell du vill.

- **Klarhet:** Ett ramverk att utvärdera andra ramverk mot — "täcker det hela pipelinen?"

- **Trygghet:** Open source, ingen vendor lock-in, inga prenumerationer
- **Nästa steg:** Kan testa direkt

## Artikel

# Lessons Learned: Running an AI Framework in a Design Team

Everyone's chasing AI tools right now. Especially in the design community.

Every week there's a new shiny thing. Generate a wireframe. Write some copy. Make an icon. Each one solves one step — brilliantly, in isolation.

Then you spend the rest of your day copy-pasting between tools, re-briefing agents, and trying to connect the dots yourself. You become the bottleneck. Having Claude draw in Figma is not agentic design — it's agent theatre.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: **most AI design tools make individuals faster, but they don't make teams better.**

Nate B. Jones nailed this — designing with AI agents is probably **slower and less effective** than designing manually, unless you manage to add a certain level of autonomy into the process. If every agent interaction requires you to hold its hand, you've just added a layer of complexity without gaining anything.

The key is a framework that lets agents work independently within guardrails — where each phase produces documents that the next agent can pick up without you re-explaining everything.

That's what Whiteport Design Studio (WDS) does. And here's what I learned running it.

---

### Lesson 1: Strategy is no longer optional for designers

AI didn't just make development cheaper. It made everything cheaper — prototyping, wireframing, content, even visual design. When producing things is effortless, **deciding what to produce becomes the real skill.**

That means every designer now carries strategic responsibility. Not just "make it look good" — but "is this the right thing to build?"

WDS forces this from day one. Phase 1 is the **Project Brief** — a focused document that answers: Why does this product exist? Who is it for? What does success look like? Your team writes this together with an AI agent called Saga, who asks the hard questions most teams skip.

This takes an hour, not a week. But it changes everything downstream. Without it, your AI agents are just generating the wrong thing faster.

This becomes possible because of AI agents. Instead of strategy being an expensive phase that only consultants do, every designer can now explore and validate strategic thinking with an agent by their side. Instead of vibe-coding something sloppy, you go into the process with a business mindset.

The Product Brief and Trigger Map become primary guardrails in the creative process. Not as limitations — but as a platform. Business goals and user needs don't constrain creativity. They focus it.

### Map user psychology before you design

Phase 2 is where WDS diverges from every other framework: **Trigger Mapping.**

Before a single screen is sketched, Saga maps the driving forces, barriers, and psychological triggers for each target group. What makes users act? What stops them? What pushes them over the edge?

This document becomes the golden thread through the entire project. Every design decision, every feature, every piece of copy traces back to a real user need — not assumptions, not "the competitor does it this way."

No other AI design framework does this.

### Lesson 3: Figma is not a design tool. It's an agent training tool.

Most designers have a fixed mindset: Figma equals design. But Figma becomes a limiting factor when agents develop applications autonomously. Gone are the days when every screen had to be beautifully laid out in a design program before anything could be built.

The role has changed. **Designers are visual instructors** — in a system where design and development should be done by agents, so humans don't become bottlenecks.

In WDS, Phase 3 is **UX Scenarios** — concrete user journeys grounded in the Trigger Map. An AI agent called Freya takes the strategic foundation and creates scenario outlines that expose every page your product needs. This isn't "user opens app, user clicks button." It's: *"Maria is frustrated because she can't find the right size — the search needs to solve that in under 10 seconds."*

Then in Phase 4, the agents wireframe and specify every screen. They write code using the design system. **Only when you don't like what you see, you open it up in Figma to make adjustments** — with the purpose of developing the design system. Not to micromanage every screen.

Each round trip to Figma makes the system smarter and more fully equipped. Eventually, the agents handle 90% of screens without you opening Figma at all.

### Lesson 4: Specifications are the new code

Sean Grove from OpenAI said it best: **"Spec is the new code."** If you haven't seen his talk, watch it — it changed how I think about the designer's role.

Phase 4 is **UX Design** — where scenarios become detailed page specifications. Layout, components, interactions, responsive behavior, accessibility. Freya works through each screen with you in different modes:

- **Dialog** — discuss what the page needs
- **Suggest** — the agent proposes, you confirm
- **Dream** — the agent creates autonomously, you review

The output isn't wireframes or mockups. It's **specifications** — detailed enough that any developer, human or AI, can build exactly what was designed. And because the specifications live in code, they open in any design application when you need to visualize.

When specs are the deliverable, designers don't hand off — they **define**. The spec is the product. The code is just a rendering of it.

### Lesson 5: Vibe coding is a dead end

Let's be honest. Vibe coding is fun. The fast prototype feels like magic. But in a professional setting, it's hot air — unless you can follow the chain of strategic logic all the way from business goal to user value.

A prototype that nobody can trace back to a real need is just a demo. It impresses in a meeting, then dies in production.

Phase 6 is **Asset Generation** — images, icons, videos, written content. But not the "generate random image" kind. Every asset is created from a specification. The AI knows what the image needs to communicate because it knows the trigger map, the scenario, and the page spec.

Strategy, not vibes. Traceability, not magic.

### Lesson 6: A design system is architecture, not a luxury

Too many teams treat the design system as a nice-to-have — something you build "when there's time." But a design system is part of the basic architecture. Without it, your agents reinvent the wheel on every screen.

In WDS, Phase 7 is the **Design System** — but you don't build it upfront and hope it fits. You build on your existing system and develop it over time, or build a new one step by step as you explore the service through your scenarios.

Every round trip to Figma feeds back into the system. Every new component the agent creates gets captured. The design system grows organically from real work — not from a theoretical workshop.

### Lesson 7: From wireframe to working code in seconds

Starting with a specification has one massive advantage over the classic way of doing things: the **unified ID**. When you start with a wireframe and a specification, you can get working code back in seconds. Real code, that can be tested and evaluated on the actual device.

Design stored as pixels doesn't bring value — it becomes a liability. Like a hot potato, nobody wants to maintain it. But wireframes and specifications can come alive instantly.

The code preserves layer IDs, so when you open it in a design application, every object can be matched and refined. The specification is the source of truth. Figma is where you give it the final polish — and those refinements feed back into the design system.

With specifications, multiple agents can collaborate — give each other feedback, test each other's code, review across models. In practice, we've seen two AI models find completely different bugs in the same code — 5 from one, 4 from another. Cross-model review catches what single-model development misses.

**From wireframe to working code. No handoff. No waiting. No lost-in-translation.**

### Lesson 8: You don't have to start from scratch

You don't need a greenfield project to use WDS. In fact, **brownfield is easier** — you have more to draw on than a blank sheet of paper. More legacy code, more existing design, more context for the agents to work with.

Phase 8 is **Product Evolution** — the full WDS pipeline in miniature. Outline your changes in scenarios. Draw. Specify. Implement with agents. Adjust in your design application if needed.

The principle is always the same, whether you're building something new or improving something old. The trigger map evolves. New scenarios emerge. The design system grows. And each round makes the system smarter.

### What this means for your team

WDS isn't another tool to chase. It is not another hype. It is a stable process that will not go out of style just because the models get better. Quite the opposite — it has the potential of providing a professional platform for all your design and development agent work, regardless of what you are building.

The framework is supported and maintained by a growing number of contributors to make sure it is optimized for new models and capabilities. And it is free.

You also have the open architecture where you can customize and add your own skills, tools, and abilities without breaking the flow.

So quite frankly, in the middle of all the confusion in the design and AI space right now — what do you have to lose? 

WDS is a **process** your team will be able to learn in a matter of hours and grow with as you follow it together together, with AI agents driving each phase and humans making the decisions.

The documents your team produces — briefs, trigger maps, scenarios, specifications, design systems — become the shared language between strategy, design, and development. No more throwing wireframes over the wall. No more "but why did we build it this way?"

Everything is traceable. Everything is connected. And everything lives in your repository — version-controlled, branchable, yours.

**Free. Open source. MIT licensed. Works with any AI model. No vendor lock-in.**
