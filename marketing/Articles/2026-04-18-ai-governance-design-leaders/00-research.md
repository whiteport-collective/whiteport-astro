# Research: AI Governance for Creative Teams — Unstuck

**Title:** AI Governance for Creative Teams
**Subtitle:** Unstuck: Unlocking the potential of design and development organizations through compliance, autonomy, and proven methodology
**Format:** Whitepaper (long-form) → 10 LinkedIn posts from different angles
**Publish:** whiteport.com (TBD: landing page with download or blog post)

## Syfte
Wake up design and development leaders to the real blocker in AI adoption: it's not the tools, it's not the training, it's the missing governance and compliance framework. Position Mårten and WDS/BMAD as the solution — someone who's been in the room with major Swedish brands and has built the infrastructure to solve this.

## Målgrupp
Design directors, head of design, VP of product, development leads, CTOs at mid-to-large companies. People who've tried to push AI adoption and hit a wall of inertia.

## Pillar
Knowledge / Provokation (hybrid — educates but also calls out the blind spot)

## Storyline

### 1. Hook — The frustration
You tried everything. Encouraged. Equipped. Sat back waiting for the productivity explosion. And... nothing happened. Three specific failure patterns:
- Designers get a working prototype from AI → redraw it in Figma
- Developers build with AI → spend more time fixing AI mistakes
- You demo AI to management → glazed eyes

### 2. The paradox
Nobody is *against* AI. The opportunity is perfectly shaped like a really sticky problem. Solo builders ship incredible things from their basements — surely that scales to teams? But it doesn't. The adoption just doesn't happen.

### 3. The pattern (credibility moment)
"The last few days, I've been invited to support three major Swedish brands across different industries and their design teams. I see the same pattern." → We are missing the WHY on an organizational scale.

### 4. The real blocker — it starts in the boardroom
Most companies get this wrong: AI adoption has to start with a document management can sign. Not enthusiasm. Not demos. A formal framework that:
- Defines what the company will do with AI
- Shields from consequences (GDPR, WCAG, incidents)
- Gives formal mandate to teams

If it's not shaped like something they can act on, your mandate to use AI will erode.

### 5. What solo builders don't need
Solo builders don't need governance. They are the governance. But in organizations, without a framework for autonomy, people hesitate. They wait. They look at you to tell them what to do. No proactive, innovative AI workflow will emerge from that.

### 6. What governance actually is (demystify)
- **Compliance**: How the company adheres to policies. What happens when there's a breach or variation.
- **Autonomy framework**: What can individuals do alone? With their team? What needs escalation?
- **Policies**: GDPR, WCAG, incident handling — down to the last team member.
- Guard rails, not handcuffs.

"Just because you give each team member a chainsaw doesn't mean the trees fall in the right direction."

### 7. What happens when it's in place
With policies, acknowledgment, and budgets:
- Teams can be sent off to solve problems autonomously
- Report back with data and numbers, not opinions
- No more walking on eggshells
- Proactive innovation instead of paralysis

### 8. The knowledge goldmine (Design Space angle)
When teams adopt AI in full swing, enormous knowledge is created. Today it's either ignored or stored in vendor catacombs you don't control. With the right infrastructure:
- Every experiment captured
- Every failed pattern recorded
- Every success documented
- Semantic, agent-readable environment that supercharges every future agent conversation

### 9. The call to action — it's on YOU, design leader
Three layers of reality:
- **Management** can green-light and turn on the language model, but they don't know what you need or what the risks are
- **Individual team members** won't take initiative without formal go-ahead and guard rails — they're responsible people
- **YOU** (the design/dev leader) are the only one who understands both the opportunity AND the organizational reality

Nobody else in the organization will initiate this. It has to be you.

### 10. Soft CTA — WDS/BMAD as the infrastructure
"I've had the honor of guiding teams in their AI adoption. I have the tools ready — agents and skills that not only create the policies and framework, but technology that uses each policy as a recipe to develop the support structure." → DM me.

## Post-Draft Analysis

### Core Arguments (extracted)

1. AI adoption fails in teams despite individual success stories
2. The blocker is organizational, not technical — governance is missing
3. Without formal framework, responsible people won't act autonomously
4. Design leaders are uniquely positioned to initiate governance
5. Governance enables autonomy (not the opposite)
6. Knowledge capture is a hidden competitive advantage

### Logic Strengths

- **Chainsaw metaphor** — immediately graspable, memorable
- **Three-layer reality** (management / individuals / you) — creates urgency by elimination
- **"Responsible people won't act"** — reframes inaction as professionalism, not laziness. Removes blame.
- **Solo builder paradox** — audience has seen this and felt confused by it. You name the gap.
- **Credibility drop** ("three Swedish brands this week") — builds authority without naming names

### Logic Weaknesses & Gaps

1. **"It starts in the boardroom" vs "It has to be you"** — slight contradiction. You say it starts with management buy-in, but then say the design leader must initiate. Clarify: the *initiative* starts with you, but you need to *shape it* so the boardroom can act on it. You're the translator.

2. **Governance definition is thin** — you list compliance, autonomy, policies. But a skeptical reader might think "that's just documentation nobody reads." You need one concrete example of what a policy *does* in practice. E.g.: "When a developer ships AI-generated code, the policy says: it must pass the same code review as human code. Simple. Now everyone knows the rule."

3. **The knowledge goldmine section** — powerful idea but comes late and feels rushed. It's almost a separate article. Risk: reader has already decided whether to act before reaching it.

4. **Missing: what happens WITHOUT governance** — you describe the frustration (nothing happens) but not the *downside risk*. What's the worst case? A GDPR breach? A PR disaster? Reputational damage from AI hallucinations in production? Adding one concrete scary example would strengthen the urgency.

5. **Missing: timeline/effort** — reader might think "great, but governance sounds like 6 months of consultant work." One line about speed would help: "This isn't a 6-month transformation project. The core framework can be in place in weeks."

6. **WDS/BMAD mention is absent** — you reference "tools I've built" but never name WDS or BMAD. Intentional? For LinkedIn this might be correct (soft sell), but for a blog article you'd want the name drop.

7. **Target audience ambiguity** — you address "design leaders" but the governance topic spans design + engineering + product + legal. A design lead reading this might think "this isn't my job." Counter: emphasize that designers understand users, risk, and ethics better than anyone — that's WHY they should own this.

### Suggested Improvements (priority order)

1. Fix the boardroom/you contradiction — one sentence bridging it
2. Add one concrete policy example (the code review one, or a GDPR one)
3. Add one scary "without governance" scenario
4. Consider splitting: LinkedIn = frustration + call to action. Blog = full framework explanation.

### Missing Argument: Governance as Autonomy Enabler (added 2026-04-18)

**The key insight the article underplays:**

Governance documents don't just cover risk — they cover the *opportunity*. What the company has to gain by *letting go of control*.

**The micro-management trap:**
- Pre-AI: micro-management was an effective (if expensive) safeguard. Spending hundreds of thousands and months on a project justified constant oversight.
- Post-AI: micro-management kills AI adoption dead. You run an agent for 30 seconds, then need to book a management meeting for approval. It doesn't fly.
- AI requires letting the agent run free — or you might as well not start.

**What governance actually enables:**
- Each person can take decisions autonomously
- Teams work things out in small groups
- No escalation needed for every AI experiment
- People know the boundaries → they move freely within them

**The startup metaphor:**
Teams need to operate like small internal startups:
- Own their problems end-to-end
- Shoulder responsibility for outcomes
- Have all prerequisites and proper information
- Make decisions fast without waiting for permission

**But this culture doesn't happen by accident.** Internal entrepreneurship needs to be built with intention, over time. Governance is the scaffolding that makes it safe to build this culture.

**The paradox to articulate in the article:**
*More structure → more freedom. More guardrails → more speed. More governance → more innovation.*

This is counter-intuitive and that's what makes it a strong argument. Most readers assume governance = bureaucracy = slowdown. The article should flip this assumption hard.

**Potential section in article:**
"Here's what governance really is: it's the company saying 'we trust you to run with this, and here are the boundaries so you can run without looking over your shoulder.' Without it, every experiment needs approval. Every AI output needs sign-off. Every decision waits for someone higher up. You've replaced the old bottleneck (manual work) with a new one (permission)."

### Missing Argument: The Soap in the Shower (added 2026-04-18)

**Metaphor:** AI is the soap in the shower — everyone knows they need it, but it's impossibly hard to pick up.

The people with the mandate (management) don't know what's needed. But they will bear ALL the consequences when things go wrong. Vague promises about productivity increases sound good in a meeting, but:
- What's the ratio now?
- How do we put that in proper perspective?
- How do we beat it into the shape of a document that management can sign without having slept last night?

**Resolving the "boardroom vs you" contradiction:**

It's not a contradiction. It's a chain:
- Management needs to sign off → but they can't write the framework themselves
- Individual designers feel the pain → but they can't get an audience with the board
- **The design leader sits in the middle.** You're the only one who can:
  1. Listen to the concerns of each designer on the floor
  2. Still get an audience in the management team
  3. Actually have the equipment to come up with a solution

You feel the pain of the *entire chain*. That's why it has to be you who suggests this move.

**What happens AFTER governance is in place:**

With policies signed, the design leader now has:
- Proper OKRs for AI adoption
- A timeline
- Clear requirements to bring to each designer

This matters because the daily work of a designer is about to look *very* different.

**The uncomfortable ask:**
It's not a small thing to require a designer to drop Figma — their main tool for years — and start designing in code. That's a fundamental shift in identity, not just tooling. You need:
- Formal backing (the governance doc)
- A clear reason *why* (the policies explain it)
- Support structure (training, pair sessions, safe-to-fail experiments)
- Time to adapt (OKRs with realistic timelines)

Without governance, this ask feels arbitrary: "the boss says use AI now." With governance, it becomes a strategic company decision with resources, support, and clear expectations.

## Interview Q&A (2026-04-18)

Raw answers from Mårten, extracted during research interview. Use to inform the whitepaper voice and arguments.

### Q: What stopped your dev team from adopting AI?

"We are too busy pouring water out of the sinking ship with buckets — we don't have time to learn how to swim."

Developers looked at AI a couple of years ago and saw "not good enough." They already had work, so why look into the future? Mårten felt the shift first because usability consultants are the first to stop getting hired when the market shifts. Consultants are the canaries in the coal mine.

Also: the dev team suffered from the exact organizational problem the article describes — no framework for change, so no change happens.

### Q: Is designer resistance different from developer resistance?

Leading a creative team is special. Highly emotional people that don't function like engineers. Many clients preferred usability consultants specifically because they were "less sensitive people." Designers want to change the world. They are fueled by creating aesthetics. Technology is not what drives them.

"Design leader" = anyone leading a company or team with creative personnel. Could be an agency or an internal design department.

Large design organizations have all the problems of enterprise, multiplied by the issues creative people bring, divided by normal tech resistance — all turned upside down by AI.

Design leaders need to use internal driving forces more than normal leaders.

### Q: Can AI speak to what drives designers?

Yes — the proposition is: you can now be in charge of not only making the model of your creation, but blowing life into it. Design, specify, develop, review.

But this only works for part of the population. Designers are extremely comfortable using Figma for everything. You have to face a real, important shift to change your tool identity.

### Q: What about people who won't change?

**The silent part (do NOT spell out in article):** Without policies, you can't force people to change. With governance, you can say: this is what management wants, this is what's rewarded, this is what I want you to succeed in.

**How to frame it instead:** When we remove unnecessary manual labor, we have more time for what really matters — quality, consistency, service to clients, and for once: actual usability testing. The thing designers always knew should be done but never got budget for.

### Q: What was the hardest part of your 8 months?

No proper client for 8+ months. Finances in ruin, borrowing money from mom to feed his son. Constant stress of "I have to do this, I don't know how, and I have nobody on my side."

But reframed: "I use machines in the gym to get stronger. Using AI to increase my mental capability is the responsible thing to do. When I see people, I meet them as the best version of me."

Feels like divine guidance from the beginning. Has built infrastructure that makes him unique — the official UX module for an open source project with thousands of users.

Should have documented the process on social media from day one, but was too embarrassed to share before being happy with the output. (Every designer relates to this.)

### Q: How does cosigning work in the framework?

The methodology isn't applied to enable production — it's put in place to change how you see the problem and prevent crappy output.

"You are allowed to do whatever you want as long as you produce this document and clear it with a colleague who cosigns it with their name."

Cosigning = shared responsibility = powerful quality mechanism. When your name is on it, you review properly. The more sponsors, the more likely you get funding to move further in the process.

### Q: What does a team actually do on Monday morning?

Start from a business problem — either assigned from above or something the team identified themselves. Describe the pain and consequences of what's not in place. The whole proposition is to reduce friction and actualize opportunity.

Like starting a micro-competition against the current solution. A/B testing with the existing business. "Here's a business problem. Come up with a better solution. Prove why it's better by showing me the numbers. You have [tight deadline]."

Insanely short deadlines. Painful, well-defined business issues. Tightly regulated resources. This forces focus, eliminates scope creep, and is measurable — which management understands.
